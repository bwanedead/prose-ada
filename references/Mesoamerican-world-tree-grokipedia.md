Mesoamerican world tree  
The Mesoamerican world tree is a foundational cosmological symbol across pre-Columbian cultures, including the Olmec, Maya, Aztec, and Mixtec, embodying the axis mundi that vertically links the underworld, earthly realm, and heavens in a structured universe. Often visualized as a ceiba tree (Ceiba pentandra) with roots extending into the subterranean domain, a straight trunk anchoring the terrestrial plane, and branches—sometimes topped by a celestial bird like the Principal Bird Deity—piercing the sky, it represents creation, fertility, renewal, and the pathway for deities, ancestors, and souls between cosmic layers.\[1\]\[2\]\[3\]  
Archaeological evidence traces the world tree motif to the Olmec period (ca. 1200–300 BCE), where it appears in cosmograms such as a small greenstone tablet depicting a central tree flanked by hearth stones symbolizing the cosmic hearth, and in architectural layouts like those at La Venta that mimic the tree's vertical axis.\[1\] This iconography persisted and evolved, as seen in the Izapa Stela 5 (ca. 300 BCE–100 CE), an early carved monument showing the tree emerging from primordial waters with a sky band above and human figures below, establishing it as a bridge across the three cosmic tiers.\[1\]\[4\] In codices and murals, such as those at San Bartolo (ca. first century BCE), the tree incorporates floral elements like the white water lily (Nymphaea ampla), emphasizing themes of life and divine emergence.\[2\]  
Among the Maya, the world tree—termed Yax Che'el Kab ("Green Tree Standing") in colonial-era texts—held profound ritual importance, as evidenced by its depiction on the sarcophagus lid of K'inich Janaab' Pakal I at Palenque (683 CE), where the ruler ascends the tree-like cross amid celestial motifs, linking royal lineage to cosmic order.\[3\]\[4\] In creation narratives like the Popol Vuh, it is erected at the world's center to support the sky, marking the "navel of the heavens" and facilitating rebirth after destruction.\[4\] For the Aztecs, the motif integrates with Nahua philosophy of cyclical time, as seen in monuments like the Tizoc Stone (ca. 1481–1486 CE), where the emperor is depicted as upholding the cosmos during the Fifth Sun era, vulnerable to earthquakes, and reflecting the teotl (sacred energy) that animates cosmic balance.\[1\]\[5\]  
The world tree's enduring significance lies in its role as a conduit for divine kingship and spiritual journeys, with rulers personifying it to assert authority—such as Maya kings adorned with tree-like regalia or Aztec leaders in rituals invoking cosmic stability—and its adaptation in colonial contexts, where Maya communities syncretized it with the Christian Tree of Life, as observed in 17th-century altars blending indigenous and European elements.\[3\]\[2\] In modern Maya communities, ceiba trees continue to mark village centers as sacred nodes, underscoring the motif's resilience in linking human existence to the cosmos.\[4\]  
Overview  
Core Concept  
The Mesoamerican world tree functions as an axis mundi, a central cosmological pillar that interconnects the three primary realms of the universe: the underworld (such as Xibalba in Maya tradition), the earthly plane, and the heavens. This vertical axis symbolizes the structural unity of the cosmos, serving as a conduit for cosmic forces and the passage between divine and mortal domains. In Mesoamerican belief systems, it represents the foundational scaffold of creation, erected at the dawn of time to organize and sustain the ordered world.\[6\]\[4\]  
Typically represented as a ceiba or kapok tree—known in Maya languages as yaxche, meaning "first" or "preeminent tree"—the world tree embodies the universe's vertical axis, with its immense height mirroring the tree's natural form that can reach up to 175 feet (53 meters). Its roots extend downward into the underworld, often depicted as penetrating watery or monstrous depths, while the trunk rises through the terrestrial realm, anchoring the human world. The branches spread upward into the sky, supporting celestial layers, and are frequently crowned by a bird figure, such as the Principal Bird Deity, signifying the pinnacle of the cosmic hierarchy. The motif appears across cultures, including early Olmec representations.\[6\]\[4\]\[1\]  
Linguistically, the concept appears in terms like wacah chan in Maya, translating to "raised-up sky tree," which emphasizes its role in elevating and stabilizing the heavens above the earth. Another variant, yax imix che, refers to the "green crocodile tree," evoking primordial imagery of the tree emerging from a foundational, serpentine or crocodilian base. These terms, rooted in pre-Hispanic vocabularies, underscore the tree's primacy as the original arboreal emblem of cosmic order.\[6\]\[3\]  
Cosmological Significance  
In Mesoamerican cosmology, the world tree functioned as the central axis mundi, positioning itself at the heart of a quadripartite universe divided into four cardinal directions, each marked by supporting trees or pillars that upheld the sky and delineated spatial order. This structure symbolized the cosmos's equilibrium, with the central tree anchoring the fivefold quincunx pattern—four peripheries surrounding a core—that integrated terrestrial, celestial, and subterranean realms. In Aztec lore, this cosmic framework intertwined with the narrative of five successive creations, or "suns," where the world tree embodied the transition from the four prior eras of destruction to the current fifth sun, Nahui Olin, representing movement and stability sustained by ritual renewal.\[3\]\[7\]  
The world tree also bore a profound association with the Milky Way, perceived as a celestial manifestation or pathway traversing the night sky, which reinforced its role as a vertical conduit linking earthly existence to divine and ancestral domains. In Maya cosmology, the Milky Way aligned seasonally to form a cosmic tree, such as on August 13 when it stood north-south, evoking the tree's upright posture and facilitating transitions between creation phases, including the descent of celestial figures. Similarly, Aztec traditions depicted the Milky Way as a bifurcated path forged during cosmogony, supported by dual world trees transformed from deities like Tezcatlipoca and Quetzalcoatl, thus partitioning the universe into directional quadrants while serving as a route to the otherworld.\[8\]  
Central to creation myths, the world tree emerged within primordial conditions of watery expanse or mud, marking the foundational separation of sky from earth and inaugurating ordered existence. In the K'iche' Maya Popol Vuh, the initial cosmos comprised a "womb of the sky" over a "placid sea," from which divine forces initiated creation, with the emergence of life symbolizing the bridging of realms post-separation. This act underscored the tree's generative power, sprouting from chaotic origins to establish vertical and horizontal cosmic divisions.\[9\]  
Temporally, the world tree served as a calendrical emblem, its branches and cycles mirroring eras of cosmic flux, destruction, and rebirth within Mesoamerican timekeeping systems. In Maya conceptions, the ceiba tree's structure aligned with the interlocking Tzolk'in (260-day ritual cycle) and Haab' (365-day solar year), forming the 52-year Calendar Round that punctuated renewal rituals, while larger Long Count increments evoked the tree's boughs as markers of katuns and baktuns—periods of potential upheaval and regeneration. This cyclical framework positioned the tree as a perpetual witness to temporal orders, ensuring continuity amid inevitable dissolutions.\[10\]  
Cultural Contexts  
Maya Representations  
In Maya cosmology, the world tree plays a pivotal role in the Popol Vuh, the K'iche' Maya sacred narrative, where it manifests as a calabash tree (Crescentia cujete) symbolizing renewal and the transition between death and life. In the myth of the Hero Twins, Hunahpu and Xbalanque, their father Hun-Hunahpu is decapitated by the lords of Xibalba, the underworld, and his head is hung in the fork of this barren tree, causing it to miraculously bear fruit resembling skulls and calabashes. This event occurs at a site associated with cosmic significance, linking the tree to the axis mundi that connects the realms, and the fruit represents the generative power of divinity. The tree's role underscores the Hero Twins' eventual descent to Xibalba, where they confront and defeat the lords, restoring cosmic balance through resurrection and maize-based creation.\[9\]\[11\]  
The Yaxche, or ceiba tree (Ceiba pentandra), serves as the primary sacred world tree in broader Maya traditions, embodying the axis mundi that pierces the three cosmic layers—underworld, earth, and sky—and facilitates communication between them. Known as "first tree of the world" or Yax Cheel Kab among the Itza Maya, it was ritually planted at creation sites to mark the world's center, often aligning with the central axis of Maya cities where temples and plazas were oriented to replicate this cosmic pillar. Its vast buttress roots were believed to descend into Xibalba, guiding souls, while its branches reached the heavens, supporting the structure of the universe.\[3\]\[11\]  
Maya kings embodied the world tree as living axes mundi, mediating between divine realms and ensuring prosperity, with royal iconography frequently depicting rulers emerging from or supporting the tree to signify their role in sustaining cosmic order. Accession rituals reinforced this symbolism, involving tree-like postures where kings assumed rigid, upright stances mimicking the tree's form during ceremonies, or the ceremonial planting of sacred trees to invoke renewal and legitimacy. Such practices positioned the ruler as the "Tree of Life made flesh," channeling vital forces from the underworld to nourish the people.\[11\]  
Variations of the world tree appear in Maya myths, contrasting the ceiba Yaxche with the maize tree, which emphasizes agricultural cycles and sustenance in creation narratives. In agricultural myths, the maize tree rises from the Maize God—often identified with Hun-Hunahpu—bearing ears of corn as fruit that symbolize human souls, forged from maize dough by the gods to animate humanity. This form highlights the tree's role in fertility and rebirth, where its produce directly links divine creation to human existence and the cyclical harvest.\[11\]\[12\]  
Aztec and Central Mexican Variants  
In Aztec cosmology, the concept of teo tlalli, or divine earth, represents the sacred foundation of the world, formed from the dismembered body of the primordial monster Cipactli, a crocodilian creature embodying chaos. According to creation accounts, the gods Quetzalcoatl and Tezcatlipoca tore Cipactli apart to establish order, with her body becoming the earth, her eyes forming caves and springs, her skin grass and flowers, and her hair trees and vegetation that anchor the cosmos.\[13\] This motif underscores the Aztec view of the earth as a living, divine entity requiring ritual nourishment to sustain cosmic balance.\[14\]  
The world tree plays a central role in the myth of the Fifth Sun, the current era of movement (nahui ollin), where the gods gathered at Teotihuacan to initiate the sun's motion. Nanahuatzin sacrificed himself by leaping into a divine fire, emerging as the sun. This structure symbolizes the axis mundi, often embodied by the Templo Mayor in Tenochtitlan, which served as a vertical conduit uniting the 13 upper levels of heaven, the earthly plane, and the nine underworld layers, with the tree's branches reaching toward celestial deities and its trunk piercing the divine earth.\[13\]\[15\]  
Ritually, the world tree manifested in the New Fire Ceremony (xiuhmolpilli), performed every 52 years to avert apocalyptic destruction and renew the cosmic cycle. Priests extinguished all fires across the empire, then ascended a scaffold resembling a great tree—adorned with paper banners and positioned on a mountain or temple—to sacrifice a victim, whose chest served as the site for drilling new fire with a wooden tool, evoking the tree's role as the axis for rebirth.\[16\] This act, tied to the Fifth Sun's sustenance, redistributed the flame to relight hearths, reaffirming the tree's function in binding time and preventing the stars (tzitzimime) from descending to end the world.\[13\]  
In neighboring Central Mexican cultures like the Mixtec, the world tree adapted as a genealogical emblem in codices, tracing rulers' lineages to divine progenitors. Mixtec manuscripts, such as the Codex Zouche-Nuttall, portray the tree as a symbolic arbor connecting mortal kings to creator gods like 1 Deer or 9 Wind, with branches representing alliances and roots divine origins, legitimizing political authority through cosmic descent.\[17\]  
Iconography and Symbolism  
Visual Motifs  
The Mesoamerican world tree is commonly depicted as a trunk, often straight or featuring deity faces, emerging from serpentine roots submerged in underworld waters, symbolizing the connection between the terrestrial realm and the subterranean domain, while its upper branches are frequently adorned with feathers representing celestial ascent. Atop the tree, a celestial bird, often resembling a quetzal or principal bird deity, perches as a mediator between the heavens and earth, emphasizing the axis mundi structure. These elements recur across various cultures, underscoring the tree's role as a conduit for cosmic forces.\[2\]\[18\]  
Stylistic variations highlight regional and temporal differences in representation. In Olmec art, the world tree is stylized as emerging from a cleft mountain, portraying it as a generative force from sacred landscape features that symbolize portals to other realms. By the Postclassic period, codices illustrate the tree integrated with directional glyphs, such as red associating with the east to denote renewal and solar emergence. These adaptations reflect evolving artistic conventions while maintaining core cosmological themes.\[19\]\[20\]  
Color symbolism enhances the tree's visual narrative, with green hues typically denoting the living, central axis of vitality and growth, often linked to the ceiba tree's lush foliage. Contrasting black for the underworld roots evokes darkness and primordial chaos, while white accents on branches or the celestial bird signify purity and the sky's expanse. Such chromatic choices align with broader Mesoamerican directional schemata, reinforcing spatial and existential hierarchies.\[21\]  
Anatomical features frequently anthropomorphize the tree, endowing it with eyes and mouths to convey sentience and interaction with the divine, as seen in various pictorial manuscripts. These humanoid traits infuse the motif with relational depth, portraying the cosmos as a living entity.\[18\]\[22\]  
Associated Elements and Deities  
In Mesoamerican cosmology, the world tree serves as a central axis mundi, often entwined by deities symbolizing celestial and terrestrial forces. Among the Aztecs, Quetzalcoatl, the feathered serpent god, is frequently depicted coiling around the tree, representing the union of sky and earth in acts of creation and renewal.\[23\] In Maya contexts, Itzamna, the creator sky god, is associated with the tree as a manifestation of divine wisdom and cosmic structure, sometimes appearing through the Vision Serpent in ritual scenes that invoke the tree's emergence from the underworld.\[23\]  
The world tree extends into four directional variants, each linked to specific colors and cardinal points that reinforce its role in partitioning the cosmos. The eastern tree is red, symbolizing dawn and vitality; the southern is yellow, evoking the sun's zenith; the western is black, tied to sunset and transition; and the northern is white, representing purity and cold.\[21\] These colored trees function as extensions of the central axis, anchoring the quadripartite universe with the green ceiba at its heart.\[21\] Variant forms of the trunk include water lilies, denoting fertile waters and rebirth, or maize cobs, embodying agricultural cycles and sustenance from the earth.\[24\]  
At the tree's summit, avian figures dominate, with the Principal Bird Deity (Itzam-Ye) perched as a celestial emblem of the sun or stars, often emerging from cosmic monsters to signify diurnal renewal.\[25\] This bird holds sprouting elements, linking it to fertility and the tree's role in cosmic transitions. At the base, underworld creatures anchor the structure, including earth crocodiles that embody the primordial watery foundation, separating the terrestrial realm from the abyss below.\[26\] These crocodilian forms, with their jaws as portals, underscore the tree's mediation between life and the subterranean depths.\[27\]  
Syncretic elements further enrich the tree's symbolism, portraying it as a shamanic conduit for journeys across realms, where jewels and flowers adorn its branches to evoke flowing cosmic energies of vitality and divine essence.\[28\] Flowers, in particular, signify blooming creation, while jewels represent stellar or precious life forces channeled through the axis.\[29\]  
Historical and Archaeological Evidence  
Early Depictions  
The earliest known depictions of world tree motifs in Mesoamerica emerged during the Olmec period (ca. 1200–400 BCE) at the ceremonial center of La Venta on the Gulf Coast of Veracruz and Tabasco. These motifs appear as abstract, stylized forms on monuments such as 25/6 and 27, which are celtiform stelae carved from green schist and gneiss, portraying the Olmec Maize God as an upright axis mundi that links the underworld, earth, and sky. Accompanying cleft symbols on these and related sculptures, such as those evoking maize ears splitting open, further emphasize themes of vegetal emergence and cosmological centrality. Similar stylized tree elements are evident in jade celts and at sites like Chalcatzingo, where banded maize cobs function as world tree proxies.\[30\]  
By the Middle to Late Formative period, world tree imagery evolved in the Izapan style (ca. 300 BCE–200 CE), particularly at the site of Izapa in Chiapas, where full tree forms began to integrate directional cosmology. Stela 5 exemplifies this development, showing a central tree with branching elements and roots that connect the three cosmic tiers—sky (topped by a jaguar mask), earth (the tree trunk with figures), and underworld (a flowing river)—while implying quadripartite spatial divisions through surrounding motifs.\[31\] This narrative relief marks a shift toward more dynamic representations, incorporating human and supernatural actors to evoke creation and directional balance, distinct from the Olmec's more symbolic abstraction.\[1\]  
During the broader Formative period (ca. 2000 BCE–250 CE), these motifs disseminated from the Olmec Gulf Coast core to highland regions, including central Mexico and the Valley of Oaxaca, shaping the iconographic foundations of emerging Maya and Zapotec traditions.\[30\] Olmec-style celts and cleft motifs appear in highland contexts like Teopantecuanitlan and Chalcatzingo, facilitating the tree's role as a unifying cosmological symbol across diverse landscapes. By the Late Preclassic (ca. 400 BCE–250 CE), depictions transitioned further from isolated abstract forms to integrated narrative scenes, with trees often framed by ritual figures and directional markers, as regional styles adapted the motif for local expressions of cosmic order.  
Key Artifacts and Sites  
One of the earliest and most prominent depictions of the Mesoamerican world tree is found on Izapa Stela 5, a monumental basalt sculpture from the Late Preclassic period, dated approximately to 300 BCE, discovered at the Izapa archaeological site in Chiapas, Mexico.\[4\] The stela features a central axis mundi represented as a world tree with branching limbs bearing fruit and leaves, flanked by human figures and a bird perched atop, suggesting themes of creation, paradise, or cosmic order.\[1\] Its roots extend downward into the underworld, while the upper branches connect to celestial elements, embodying the tree's role as a bridge between realms.\[4\]  
In the Classic Maya period, the sarcophagus lid from the Temple of the Inscriptions at Palenque, Chiapas, Mexico, dated to 683 CE, presents a iconic image of the world tree in a royal funerary context.\[32\] Carved for K'inich Janaab' Pakal the Great, the lid shows the ruler emerging from the underworld, with a ceiba-like world tree sprouting from his body, its branches adorned with celestial motifs and a bird of creation at the apex, symbolizing the king's ascent to divine status and the cyclical renewal of life.\[32\] The tree's roots delve into the maw of an earth monster below, reinforcing its function as a conduit between the underworld, earthly realm, and heavens.\[32\]  
Postclassic Maya codices provide painted illustrations of the world tree integrated into ritual and astronomical contexts. The Dresden Codex, a bark-paper manuscript from the Yucatán region dating to after 1200 CE, includes depictions on several pages of ceiba trees emerging from sacrificial figures or deities, often linked to calendrical almanacs and cosmic events, such as a tree growing from the chest of a victim to signify renewal and divine sustenance.\[33\] These images associate the tree with gods like Itzamna, emphasizing its role in maintaining universal harmony.\[4\] Similarly, the Borgia Codex, a Central Mexican ritual manuscript from the late Postclassic period (ca. 1400–1500 CE), features sacred trees in its directional almanacs, such as on Plate 49, where ceiba trees mark the eastern quadrant, accompanied by deities and offerings that evoke the world tree's stabilizing presence in cosmology.\[21\] These codical trees, often personified or stylized, served as tools for divination and ritual prognostication.\[34\]  
Archaeological sites across Mesoamerica incorporate real ceiba trees (Ceiba pentandra) as living embodiments of the world tree within ceremonial centers. At Tikal, Guatemala, a major Classic Maya city (ca. 200–900 CE), massive ceiba trees, known as yax che or "green tree," were integrated into the urban landscape around temples and plazas, their towering forms up to 70 meters tall symbolizing the axis mundi and serving as focal points for rituals connecting the living world to ancestors and deities.\[35\] In Tenochtitlan, the Aztec capital (ca. 1325–1521 CE), sacred trees featured in ceremonial precincts near the Templo Mayor, where they were planted or incorporated in association with sacred landscapes, representing the primordial tree that supported the five cosmic eras and facilitated offerings to gods like Huitzilopochtli.\[36\] In 2012, archaeologists discovered the trunk of a sacred tree, dated to ca. 1440 CE, at the base of the Templo Mayor, likely used in rituals symbolizing cosmic pillars.\[37\] These natural analogs underscore the world tree's enduring physical and spiritual presence in Mesoamerican sacred spaces.\[33\]  
Modern Interpretations  
Scholarly Perspectives  
Scholars have debated the precise botanical prototype of the Mesoamerican world tree, with prominent interpretations favoring the ceiba tree (Ceiba pentandra), maize (Zea mays), or the water lily (Nymphaea ampla), each supported by varying iconographic and ethnohistoric evidence. The ceiba, a towering kapok tree native to lowland forests, is often identified due to its depiction in Maya art as a vertical axis connecting realms, as seen in stelae and ceramics where its broad trunk and branching form symbolize cosmic structure.\[2\] In contrast, maize emerges in ethnohistoric accounts and glyphs as a sacred plant embodying fertility and renewal, with world tree motifs sometimes incorporating maize cobs or foliation to evoke agricultural cycles central to Maya worldview.\[38\] Recent botanical analyses, however, propose the water lily as the primary model, citing its emergent growth from aquatic depths, white blossoms evoking purity, and associations with deities in San Bartolo murals (ca. first century B.C.), which align with ethnohistoric records of rulers personifying floral world trees in rituals.\[2\]  
The functional roles attributed to the world tree in scholarly theories emphasize its multifaceted symbolism in cosmology and society. As a shamanic ladder, it facilitated visionary ascent between underworld, earth, and sky, enabling rulers to access divine knowledge during bloodletting rites, as evidenced in Palenque iconography where kings embody the tree's emergence.\[39\] It also served as a potent emblem of royal legitimacy, with Maya stelae portraying rulers as anthropomorphic trees to assert divine ancestry and cosmic order, reinforcing dynastic authority through mythic reenactments of creation.\[40\] Additionally, the tree functioned as an astronomical marker, aligning with solstices and equinoxes in site orientations like those at Copán, where architectural features and glyphs link it to calendrical cycles and celestial events.\[40\]  
Significant gaps persist in understanding the world tree's mythic narratives, largely due to disruptions from the Spanish conquest, which destroyed codices and suppressed indigenous oral traditions, leaving fragmented accounts in colonial-era ethnohistories.\[41\] Proposed influences from Asian or African tree myths, such as parallels to Norse Yggdrasil or Egyptian motifs, remain debated but lack archaeological or linguistic substantiation, with most experts attributing the motif to indigenous Mesoamerican developments.\[42\]  
Key contributions to these perspectives include Mary Miller's analyses of iconography in The Blood of Kings (1986, co-authored with Linda Schele), which elucidate the tree's role in dynastic art as a conduit for ritual power, and Linda Schele's work in Maya Cosmos (1993, with David Freidel and Joy Parker), which reconstructs its cosmological functions through epigraphic and shamanic lenses.\[43\]\[39\] These studies highlight ongoing interpretive challenges, such as reconciling disparate artifact depictions like Izapa Stela 5 with broader mythic frameworks.\[40\]  
Contemporary Cultural Impact  
In contemporary Maya communities in Guatemala and Mexico, the ceiba tree retains its sacred status as the world tree, serving as a central element in rituals that connect the earthly realm with spiritual dimensions. Modern practitioners incorporate ceiba trees into ceremonies for rain invocation and agricultural blessings, often gathering at their bases to perform offerings that echo ancient traditions while addressing current environmental needs.\[44\] Additionally, parts of the ceiba, such as its bark, are utilized in traditional healing practices for conditions like diabetes and inflammation, integrating its mythological significance with ethnomedical applications.\[45\]  
The motif of the Mesoamerican world tree has influenced modern art and literature, drawing on themes from sacred texts like the Popol Vuh to explore indigenous identity and cosmology. In literature, Miguel Ángel Asturias's novel Men of Maize (1949) weaves Popol Vuh-inspired narratives, portraying maize and arboreal symbols as embodiments of Maya creation myths and resistance to cultural erasure.\[46\] In visual art, contemporary murals such as those by Mexican artist Miguel Contreras reinterpret the world tree as a unifying cosmological axis, blending pre-Columbian iconography with modern socio-political commentary on indigenous heritage.\[47\]  
In global popular culture, the world tree appears as a symbol of ancient wisdom and interconnected realms in media inspired by Mesoamerican lore. The Netflix animated series Maya and the Three (2021) features ceiba-like trees as pivotal environmental and narrative elements in its epic tale of Maya mythology, highlighting themes of destiny and harmony with nature.\[48\] Similarly, adventure video games with Mesoamerican settings, such as Itorah (2022), incorporate world tree motifs as sacred landscapes that guide exploration and evoke cultural depth.\[49\]  
The ceiba tree's symbolism has been embraced by modern eco-movements in Mesoamerica, linking its sacred role to conservation initiatives that protect biodiversity and combat deforestation. In Yucatán, Mexico, reforestation projects celebrate the ceiba as a keystone species, promoting its planting to restore ecosystems while honoring its spiritual legacy among indigenous groups.\[50\] Organizations like the Ceiba Foundation for Tropical Conservation advocate for habitat corridors that safeguard ceiba populations, emphasizing their ecological value in carbon sequestration and wildlife support alongside cultural reverence.\[51\]
