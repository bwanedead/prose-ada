\#\#\# Cellular Automata in Wolfram's Framework

Cellular automata (CA) are discrete mathematical models consisting of a grid of cells, each in a finite state (e.g., 0 or 1, black or white), evolving over discrete time steps according to local rules based on neighboring cells. Wolfram's work, starting in the early 1980s, formalized and expanded CA as tools for studying complexity, demonstrating that simple rules can generate intricate, unpredictable patterns from ordered initial conditions. 

In one-dimensional elementary CA, cells update based on their state and two nearest neighbors, yielding 256 possible rules (numbered 0-255) due to 2^8 combinations of inputs.  Wolfram classified these into four behaviors: Class 1 (evolve to uniform state), Class 2 (periodic patterns), Class 3 (chaotic, aperiodic), and Class 4 (localized structures with long-range interactions, computationally universal). Rule 30 exemplifies Class 3: starting from a single black cell, it produces apparent randomness on the left, structured triangles on the right, embodying computational irreducibility—outcomes cannot be predicted faster than simulation. 

Rule 110 (Class 4\) is Turing-complete, capable of universal computation, proven by Matthew Cook in 2004 under Wolfram's direction. Two-dimensional CA, like Conway's Game of Life, extend this: cells live or die based on neighbor counts, yielding gliders and oscillators.  Wolfram's \*A New Kind of Science\* (2002) argues CA reveal a paradigm where complexity arises from simple rules, challenging traditional equation-based science and suggesting nature operates via discrete computations.  This includes statistical mechanics interpretations, where CA model self-organization and entropy. 

\#\#\# Multiway Systems and Graphs

Multiway systems generalize single-threaded CA by allowing multiple possible updates at each step, branching into all sequences of rule applications.  Represented as multiway graphs, nodes are states, edges are updates; paths trace histories, with branching for nondeterminism and merging for confluence. 

For string substitution rules like {A → BBB, BB → A}, multiway graphs exhibit emergent geometry, such as fractal dimensions ≈1.59 or 1.69.  Causal invariance—where different update orders yield equivalent outcomes—ensures reconvergence, analogous to relativity's light cones. Branchial graphs slice multiway graphs transversely, connecting entangled states (sharing ancestors), modeling quantum superposition. 

Multiway causal graphs overlay causal edges (dependencies between events) on multiway structures, capturing intra- and inter-branch relations.  Extensions include multiway Turing machines, combinators, and number-based systems, where iterations like n → {a n \+ b, c n \+ d} yield complex graphs.  In rule-space multiway systems, all possible rules apply simultaneously, forming a rulial graph encompassing all computations.

\#\#\# Networked History of the Universe

Wolfram's Physics Project (launched 2020\) models the universe as a spatial hypergraph: nodes are abstract elements ("atoms of space"), hyperedges are relations; rules rewrite hypergraphs, generating spacetime and matter.   Evolution starts from minimal hypergraphs, applying rules like {{x, y, z} → {w, w, y}, {w, x, z}}, producing emergent dimensionality (e.g., 3D space from connectivity). 

Multiway systems integrate: nondeterministic updates branch histories, multiway graphs represent quantum paths, branchial graphs yield entanglement.  Causal graphs track event dependencies, deriving special relativity (causal invariance implies Lorentz transformations) and general relativity (curvature from hypergraph density).  Elementary scales: length ℓ (node separation), time τ (update step), energy ε (causal edge flux); Planck units emerge from limits like c \= ℓ/τ.

The ruliad—limit of all rules—encompasses the universe; observers (persistent structures) perceive slices, explaining laws as observer-dependent.  This resolves existence: the universe is a computational sample of the ruliad.

\#\#\# Interconnections and Implications

CA provided the foundation: their irreducibility and universality inspired exploring computational universes, leading to multiway systems for nondeterminism.  Hypergraphs generalize CA grids, multiway graphs extend branching, modeling quantum-relativistic physics.  The networked history views spacetime as emergent from causal-event networks, particles as persistent hypergraph tangles. 

Critiques note lack of empirical predictions, but the project (ongoing as of 2026\) yields tools for simulation and derives known physics.  Implications: unified theory via computation, observer role in laws, potential for dark matter as hypergraph defects.
