# **Strange Attractors**

A strange attractor is a fractal structure in the phase space of a dynamical system that attracts nearby trajectories while exhibiting chaotic behavior, characterized by sensitive dependence on initial conditions and a non-integer Hausdorff dimension.\[1\] These attractors represent bounded regions where system trajectories remain confined indefinitely, yet never repeat periodically, forming intricate, self-similar patterns that reveal the underlying complexity of nonlinear dynamics.\[2\] The concept was first introduced by mathematicians David Ruelle and Floris Takens in 1971 to describe turbulent phenomena in fluid dynamics, distinguishing them from conventional attractors like fixed points or limit cycles by the presence of expansion in unstable directions leading to exponential divergence of nearby paths.  
Strange attractors are central to chaos theory, emerging in dissipative systems where energy dissipation balances forcing, resulting in low-dimensional chaos observable in dimensions as low as two for maps and three for flows.\[3\] A seminal example is the Lorenz attractor, discovered by meteorologist Edward Lorenz in 1963 through numerical simulations of atmospheric convection; it arises from a system of three ordinary differential equations and displays butterfly-shaped trajectories that never cross but densely fill a fractal manifold of dimension approximately 2.06.\[3\] Other notable strange attractors include the Hénon attractor (1976), generated by a two-dimensional quadratic map that stretches and folds phase space to produce a fractal of dimension about 1.26, and the Rössler attractor (1976), a three-dimensional flow system yielding a single-loop chaotic structure with dimension roughly 2.01, both serving as simplified models for studying chaotic transitions.\[4\]\[5\]  
Key properties of strange attractors include positive topological entropy, indicating infinite unstable periodic orbits dense within the structure, and ergodicity, where time averages equal space averages over the attractor.\[1\] They underpin applications across disciplines, from weather prediction and fluid turbulence to biological rhythms and electronic circuits, highlighting how deterministic equations can yield unpredictable yet structured outcomes.\[3\]

## **Production history**

### **Development and writing**

The episode "Strange Attractors" was written by Juan Carlos Coto, a co-executive producer on *Heroes* who contributed several scripts to season 4, emphasizing themes of character isolation and the far-reaching consequences of supernatural abilities.\[6\] Coto's work on the series highlighted the struggles of individuals with extraordinary powers navigating everyday life, a core motif of the post-2007–2008 Writers Guild strike reboot that prioritized standalone episodes amid a larger seasonal narrative.  
The development of the script integrated into season 4's broader arc, where characters with abilities pursue normalcy while grappling with their identities, with the title "Strange Attractors" alluding to the chaos theory concept of complex systems exhibiting unpredictable yet bounded behavior, metaphorically reflecting the erratic trajectories of the protagonists' lives. Specific plot decisions included expanding the storyline of Jeremy Greer, a teenager whose involuntary ability to drain life force through physical contact leads to accidental harm and confrontation with mob justice, serving as a self-contained tale that bridges the season's carnival community subplot and Claire Bennet's college experiences.\[7\]  
Produced under code 407, the episode aligned its air date with October 26, 2009, fitting the fall broadcast schedule to capitalize on Halloween-themed elements like sorority hazing and supernatural mishaps. This structure allowed "Strange Attractors" to advance personal stakes for characters like Noah Bennet and Tracy Strauss without resolving major arcs, maintaining momentum toward the season's exploration of community and control among the powered.\[8\]

### **Filming and post-production**

"Strange Attractors" was directed by Tucker Gates, who brought his experience in building dramatic tension within ensemble narratives to the episode's multifaceted storylines.\[9\]  
Principal filming for the series, including this installment, occurred in Los Angeles and Santa Clarita, California, utilizing urban and industrial sites to convey realistic settings for the characters' conflicts.\[10\]  
In post-production, the episode featured visual effects to depict supernatural elements, such as possession sequences and ability manifestations, handled by the show's effects team to integrate seamlessly with live-action footage. Sound design emphasized eerie, otherworldly audio cues for moments of psychological torment, enhancing the atmospheric tension.\[11\]

## **Episode summary**

### **Plot synopsis**

The episode opens with Claire Bennet and her roommate Gretchen participating in a sorority hazing ritual at Arlington University, where they are blindfolded and taken to an abandoned slaughterhouse as part of a scavenger hunt. While navigating the eerie location and discussing their recent kiss and Claire's desire for a normal life, they encounter clues involving bloodied lockers and cryptic messages. An invisible assailant, revealed to be sorority sister Becky Taylor with the power of invisibility, attacks Gretchen, choking her with an unseen force. Claire intervenes, fighting off the attacker and suffering a severe impalement on a rebar stake, which she heals from almost instantly in front of the other pledges. This public demonstration of her invulnerability shocks the witnesses, including Gretchen, as Becky flees the scene.\[18\]  
Meanwhile, Matt Parkman struggles with Sylar's possession of his mind after previously using his telepathy to trap the killer within himself. In Los Angeles, Sylar taunts Matt and briefly takes control during an intimate moment with Matt's wife, Janice, prompting Matt to warn her of the danger and urge her to leave with their son for safety. Desperate to suppress Sylar, Matt discovers that alcohol affects the entity, causing pain with each drink, and consumes large quantities of tequila to black out and seemingly expel him. However, this is a ruse; Sylar regains full control of Matt's body while he is unconscious, leaving the real Matt trapped and helpless as Sylar resumes his manipulations.\[18\]  
In Cainan, Georgia, Noah Bennet attempts to secure the release of Jeremy Greer, a troubled teenager with the ability to involuntarily drain life force from others when emotionally overwhelmed, who has been arrested following the apparent accidental deaths of his parents from carbon monoxide poisoning—a scenario Noah had staged to protect him. Partnering with Tracy Strauss, who poses as Jeremy's aunt to gain access, Noah and Tracy counsel the boy on controlling his powers by sharing Tracy's own experiences with emotional triggers amplifying abilities. Despite initial success in arranging Jeremy's release with a new identity, an angry mob confronts him outside the station, leading to Jeremy accidentally killing a man by draining his life. Overwhelmed, Jeremy loses control amid the violence, but rogue police officers chain him to a truck and execute him by dragging, leaving his body in the street. Devastated, Tracy confronts Noah about their failure before driving away, guided by a mysterious compass pointing toward a carnival community for people with abilities.\[18\]  
The storylines intersect through teases of the carnival as a haven for specials: Tracy is briefly transported there by Samuel Sullivan, who shows her the grounds and offers belonging among others like her, while Sylar—now in Matt's body—experiences false memories tied to the location. The episode climaxes with Samuel, enraged by Jeremy's death, arriving at the Cainan police station and using his terrakinetic powers to demolish the entire building in a vengeful display of destruction.\[18\]

### **Character developments**

In the episode "Strange Attractors," Claire Bennet's arc centers on the disruption of her attempt to maintain a facade of normal college life, as a sorority hazing ritual in an abandoned slaughterhouse forces her to confront personal vulnerabilities and the inescapability of her extraordinary abilities. During the ordeal, Claire discusses her recent kiss with roommate Gretchen, acknowledging potential romantic feelings rather than dismissing them, which strains their friendship while highlighting her ongoing identity crisis amid peer pressures and hidden powers. This culminates in the exposure of sorority member Becky's invisibility ability, reinforcing Claire's realization that abnormality permeates even her "fresh start" environment.\[8\]\[7\]  
Matt Parkman's storyline advances his internal struggle against Sylar's possession, as he experiments with alcohol to suppress the intruder's influence by damaging brain cells, portraying addiction as a desperate tool for regaining mental autonomy. This tactic temporarily weakens Sylar but ultimately backfires, allowing the killer full control and leading Parkman to isolate himself from his family to protect them, evident in his confession of "demons" that prompts his wife to leave. The arc underscores Parkman's psychological torment and loss of agency, emphasizing themes of control and relational fallout.\[8\]\[7\]  
Noah Bennet and Tracy Strauss's arcs intertwine in their failed attempt to aid teenager Jeremy Greer, questioning Noah's paternalistic interventions as he manipulates circumstances to secure Jeremy's release, only for the effort to collapse amid escalating violence. Tracy, initially partnering with Noah by posing as Jeremy's aunt and sharing her own history of emotional power loss, rejects mainstream integration for powered individuals, shifting allegiance to Samuel Sullivan's carnival after witnessing systemic rejection. This pivot highlights Tracy's growing isolation and preference for a community of outcasts over normalized society.\[8\]\[7\]  
Jeremy Greer's brief but pivotal arc illustrates the tragic consequences of uncontrolled powers, as his emotional instability leads to accidental killings during a press event, inciting mob fear and police overreaction that chains him and results in his fatal shooting. This sequence exposes systemic failures in protecting young powered individuals, amplifying themes of societal persecution and the inescapability of innate abilities.\[8\]\[7\]  
Samuel Sullivan's character emerges with deepening villainy through his calculated rage, as he orchestrates the destruction of a police station in retaliation for Jeremy's death, blending paternal manipulation with destructive power to recruit allies like Tracy. His actions reveal a complex antagonism, driven by a vision of communal belonging for the powered, yet marked by extreme responses that position him as a Magneto-like figure.\[8\]\[7\]

## **Broadcast and reception**

### **Airing details and viewership**

"Strange Attractors," the sixth episode of the fourth season of the NBC series *Heroes*, originally premiered in the United States on October 26, 2009, airing in the network's Monday night time slot at 9:00 p.m. ET/PT.\[9\] The episode carried production code 407 and had a standard runtime of 43 minutes.\[19\]  
In terms of viewership, the episode attracted 5.7 million U.S. viewers,\[20\] aligning with season 4's broader trend of declining ratings after the higher averages of season 2 (around 11 million viewers per episode) and season 3 (approximately 7.7 million). This drop reflected challenges in maintaining audience momentum following the writers' strike-impacted season 2 and the ambitious narrative shifts in season 3\.  
The episode was included in the *Heroes: Season 4* DVD release on August 3, 2010, as part of the complete season set available for home media purchase.\[21\] Internationally, airing schedules varied; for instance, it broadcast in Australia on November 25, 2009, via the Sci Fi Channel, and in the United Kingdom on February 7, 2010, on Syfy.\[22\]  
As of 2024, "Strange Attractors" is available for streaming as part of the full *Heroes* series on Peacock, NBCUniversal's platform, which assumed distribution rights under the company's ongoing media consolidation efforts.

### **Critical and audience response**

The episode "Strange Attractors" from the television series *Heroes* received mixed responses from critics and audiences, often highlighted for its thematic nod to chaos theory amid uneven narrative execution.\[8\]\[7\]  
Robert Canning of IGN awarded the episode a 7.0 out of 10, praising the tensions in its subplots—particularly the depth added to the carnival storyline and Claire Bennet's exploration of identity—while critiquing the pacing issues across multiple arcs, which felt rushed and disjointed, preventing deeper development of horror or thematic elements.\[8\] In contrast, Steve Heisler of The A.V. Club gave it a D+ grade, lambasting the disjointed storytelling and lack of momentum in the season, noting that predictable plots and unresolved threads, such as those involving Claire's teen drama and the Bennet-Tracy efforts, rendered the episode meandering and devoid of meaningful progress.\[7\]  
Audience reception mirrored this ambivalence, with an IMDb user rating of 7.1 out of 10 based on 981 votes (as of 2024), reflecting appreciation for character moments like Claire's personal revelations and the emotional weight of Jeremy's arc, though many lamented the underutilization of Jeremy's storyline and the episode's failure to advance key season threads.\[9\] Overall, viewers and reviewers perceived "Strange Attractors" as mid-season filler, with some commending the title's tie-in to chaos theory as a metaphor for the show's unpredictable plots, but others faulted it for prioritizing subplots over main narrative propulsion.\[8\]\[7\] Post-cancellation discussions among fans remain limited, with sparse long-term analysis on platforms like Reddit, focusing instead on standout elements like the Jeremy narrative rather than comprehensive episode reevaluation.\[23\]
