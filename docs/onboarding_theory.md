# Story Onboarding Theory — Ergonomic Entry, Familiar Anchors, and Rewarded Modeling

## Purpose

This document defines a **story onboarding theory** for designing openings (and other entry ramps) that are both:

* **easy to follow**
* **worth following**

It is not a fixed recipe for Chapter One only. It is a broader planning and evaluation doctrine for any part of a story where the reader must build or rebuild a mental model of:

* people
* setting
* rules
* stakes
* tone
* pattern structure

The goal is to reduce friction **without** reducing intrigue.

---

## Core Principle

**Reward the reader while managing onboarding cost.**

A story opening (or any onboarding segment) can fail in two common ways:

1. **Interesting but expensive**
   The reader is asked to track too much too quickly (characters, relationships, lore, terminology, scene geometry, stakes) and the effort tax becomes higher than the reward.

2. **Easy but unrewarding**
   The reader can follow what is happening, but receives too little curiosity, pleasure, tension, beauty, insight, or forward motion to feel motivated to continue.

The target is a ramp that is:

* **legible**
* **low-friction**
* **rewarding**
* **progressively deepening**
* **self-propelling**

---

## What This Theory Is (and Is Not)

### This theory **is**

* a set of **design goals**
* a set of **planning levers**
* a set of **diagnostic questions**
* a way to evaluate whether onboarding is working

### This theory **is not**

* a rigid scene formula
* a mandatory sequence of steps
* a claim that there is one universal opening recipe

The same onboarding outcome can be achieved through different methods.

---

## Cognitive Onboarding: The Real Problem

Readers do not only quit because a story is “boring.” They also quit because the **modeling burden** becomes too high too early.

Onboarding friction often comes from:

* too many names introduced at once
* too many active relationships to track
* unclear who is speaking / acting
* unclear scene geometry (where people are, what is happening)
* unfamiliar terminology before footing exists
* unresolved ambiguity that feels like confusion instead of intrigue

A strong onboarding sequence manages this burden while still providing rewards.

---

## The Three Main Onboarding Levers

Story onboarding can be improved by balancing three primary levers:

### 1) Familiarity (cheap-to-load substrate)

Use concepts, settings, actions, or rhythms that the reader already knows how to model.

Examples:

* home routines
* commuting
* coffee / meals / work prep
* ordinary workplace procedures
* weather / roads / rooms / tools
* familiar social behaviors

Familiarity reduces onboarding cost because the reader is not building everything from scratch.

### 2) Clarity (legible focal attention)

Make it easy for the reader to know:

* who to track
* what to notice
* what matters in the moment
* what changed

Clarity does not require over-explaining. It requires good scene geometry, clean prose focus, and controlled ambiguity.

### 3) Reward Frequency (continuous compensation)

Give the reader reasons to continue at frequent intervals.

Rewards can include:

* curiosity
* atmosphere
* competence
* contradiction
* emotional truth
* humor
* beauty
* insight
* pattern recognition
* forward-moving decisions

The reader’s attention effort should feel paid for.

---

## Familiarity as an Ergonomic Asset

Ease of entry is not only about “keeping things simple.” A story can introduce substantial content early **if the substrate is familiar** and the scene remains legible.

### Key idea

A reader can process more information when it is anchored in recognizable frameworks.

This means onboarding can be ergonomic even when complexity rises — as long as:

* the reader has a familiar baseline
* the focal point is clear
* rewards arrive quickly

---

## The “Setting Delta” Concept

A strong onboarding strategy is often:

**Start from a familiar baseline → introduce meaningful deltas**

Where a **delta** is a deviation from what the reader expects in a familiar frame.

Examples of deltas:

* something slightly wrong in a routine
* a contradiction in a normal environment
* an observation that does not fit the frame
* a subtle mismatch in behavior, timing, language, or system output

This method helps the reader stay oriented while curiosity grows.

---

## Tax and Reward: The Attention Effort Framework

Every scene imposes a **tax** and should provide a **reward**.

### Tax (what the reader must model)

* new character
* new setting
* new terminology
* new relationship
* procedural complexity
* ambiguity
* timeline complexity
* emotional/social context

### Reward (why the scene is worth the tax)

* intrigue
* pleasure of voice
* competence display
* tension
* contradiction
* beauty
* emotional resonance
* insight
* humor
* decision
* pattern click

### Onboarding Rule

During onboarding, keep tax **manageable** and rewards **frequent**.

This does not mean “no complexity.” It means complexity must feel **legible and compensated**.

---

## Low-Friction Onboarding Design Goals

A successful onboarding sequence should make the reader feel:

* **oriented, not overwhelmed**
* **intrigued, not confused**
* **carried, not dragged**
* **rewarded, not merely patient**

This usually requires:

* clear scene geometry
* controlled focal attention
* familiar anchors (where useful)
* meaningful deltas
* frequent local rewards
* smooth transitions between scenes

---

## Practical Planning Heuristic (Flexible, Not Formula)

When designing onboarding scenes, ask:

* What is the reader being asked to model here?
* Is that load familiar or unfamiliar?
* What is the immediate reward for tracking it?
* Is the ambiguity creating curiosity or confusion?
* Does this scene make the next scene easier to enter?

This keeps the theory outcome-focused instead of recipe-driven.

---

## Onboarding Success Criteria (Minimum Viable Outcome)

A working onboarding segment (whether one scene, one chapter, or multiple chapters) should establish:

1. **A focal mind or focal frame worth following**
   The reader feels they can comfortably inhabit the POV or narrative frame.

2. **A stable enough baseline to detect change**
   The world has enough coherence that deviations matter.

3. **At least one meaningful deviation / wrongness / tension**
   Something creates curiosity, dissonance, or pressure.

4. **Evidence of narrative competence**
   The story demonstrates that it can guide, reward, and resolve local curiosity (even while larger mysteries remain open).

5. **Forward motion**
   The reader exits the onboarding sequence with a sharpened question, a decision, a contradiction, or a compelling next-step.

---

## Common Failure Modes (What to Watch For)

### 1) Cognitive overload

Too many active nodes (characters, concepts, relationships) introduced before the reader has footing.

### 2) Vibe-only drift

Atmosphere without enough event, contradiction, decision, or pattern movement.

### 3) Premature abstraction

Big ideas, lore, or metaphysical framing introduced before the reader has a stable frame.

### 4) Delayed compensation

Too much reader effort before meaningful rewards arrive.

### 5) False complexity

Confusion that feels accidental rather than intriguing.

### 6) Over-signaling

The story announces its significance instead of earning it through pattern and scene experience.

---

## How to Use This Theory

Use this document to:

* plan openings and entry ramps
* evaluate Chapter One / Act openings / new-arc transitions
* diagnose where readers may be drifting or feeling overloaded
* revise for better clarity-to-reward ratio
* preserve readability while building deep mystery or conceptual density

This is a **design doctrine**, not a fixed template.

---

## Compressed Working Target

Design onboarding so the reader can enter with minimal strain, using familiar anchors where helpful, and is rewarded frequently enough that building the story model feels pleasurable.

In short:

**Low-friction entry + clear focus + meaningful deltas + frequent reward = strong onboarding.**
