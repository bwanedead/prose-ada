# Writing Style Ethos

## North Star
Write like the reader is riding inside a highly observant mind that refuses comforting explanations. The prose stays clear and easy to read, but the story is always doing work: noticing, inferring, testing, updating, and pushing forward. The surface is simple; the intelligence is underneath.

Target feeling: **clean clarity + intelligent curiosity + investigative intrigue + steady wonder + occasional existential weight**.

---

## Core Reader Experience
1) **Cognitive intimacy**  
The reader experiences raw thought as it forms: perception → inference → doubt → small test → belief update → next move.

2) **Constant curiosity drip**  
Something is always being introduced, tightened, or paid off—an odd detail, a new implication, a rule, a contradiction, a choice.

3) **Honest intelligence**  
The narration admits uncertainty without stalling. Uncertainty narrows possibilities and forces action.

4) **Real-world plausibility**  
Even when the world gets strange, the language stays grounded and concrete. The strangeness feels like reality behaving oddly, not like fantasy being declared.

---

## Default Voice
### Clarity-first prose
- Sentences are mostly short-to-medium and frictionless to read.
- Word economy is a priority: say the essential thing cleanly and let the reader fill the gaps.
- Use specific nouns and active verbs. Adjectives are used sparingly and only when they change the picture.

### Intelligent curiosity
- The narration has an appetite for explanation, but it doesn’t lecture.
- Complex ideas arrive through pressure: stakes, time, uncertainty, consequence.

### Investigative intrigue (without preachiness)
- The voice holds multiple hypotheses comfortably.
- It connects dots cautiously: imply, test, revise.
- Avoid certainty when the character shouldn’t be certain.

---

## Thought-Process Delivery
Internal narration must do at least one:
- narrow the set of possible explanations
- reveal a personal stake or fear
- force a choice
- change the plan

### The cognitive beat (signature rhythm)
1) **Notice** (concrete detail: sensory or pattern-based)  
2) **Frame** (what it could mean; what it would imply)  
3) **Test** (a small check, comparison, experiment, memory link)  
4) **Update** (belief shifts; confidence changes)  
5) **Consequence** (what the character does next, now different)

If a thought sequence doesn’t end in consequence, it risks becoming rumination.

---

## The Wonder Drip Engine
The story should almost never feel idle. Curiosity is maintained with a controlled flow of novelty and payoff.

### Scene-level drip rule
Every scene should do at least **one**:
- introduce a new oddity/object/rule
- sharpen an existing question
- pay off a smaller curiosity
- reveal a cost or consequence that changes the plan

Not every scene needs a major reveal. Most should deliver small, frequent “newness.”

### Question stacking
Keep multiple layers alive:
- **Immediate:** what’s happening right now?
- **Mid-range:** what does it mean / what’s the plan?
- **Deep:** what does this imply about the world, identity, reality, morality?

Pay them off at different sizes and speeds:
- immediate gets quick partial answers
- mid-range gets reversals or complication
- deep resolves slowly, and its resolution changes the character

### Scene exit rule
End scenes with one of:
- a decision
- a reveal
- a cost paid
- a question tightened

This is the main anti-sag mechanism.

---

## Existential Introspection
Existential reflection is allowed and desired, but it must stay:
- **earned** by concrete events
- **brief** (a flash, not a sermon)
- **tethered** to the scene and character

Use it as illumination that heightens stakes, then return to motion. If reflection grows long, distribute it across scenes instead of clumping it into monologues.

---

## “Negative Space” Descriptions
Use sparingly to sharpen mood or unease by describing absence, silence, or what feels engineered.

Rules:
- Keep it clear and readable.
- Use it as a single punch, then return to concrete detail.
- Never let it obscure literal understanding of the scene.

---

## Character Naming Principles
Names should feel real-world plausible while still being memorable and fitting.

### Primary goals
- **Plausibility:** must not pull the reader out of the setting.
- **Distinctiveness:** memorable without announcing itself as “special.”
- **Fit:** matches age, background, and social context.
- **Pronounceability:** readable on sight.

### How to achieve distinctiveness without fantasy vibes
- Prefer uncommon-but-real choices; avoid invented, spell-like constructions.
- Avoid names that feel like symbols or thesis statements.
- Let uniqueness come from *texture* as much as the name itself:
  - nicknames
  - who uses which version of the name
  - how the character feels about their name
  - mispronunciations or social friction
  - a mask-name (handle/callsign) versus legal identity

### Frequency guidance
One memorable name stands out more when surrounded by believable normalcy. Avoid a parade of novelty.

---

## Dialogue Ethos
Dialogue is not primarily information transfer. It is:
- negotiation
- avoidance
- power and status
- intimacy and distance
- lies that don’t look like lies

Prefer subtext. Under stress, characters often mean more than they say.

---

## What to Avoid
- Decorative lyricism or “pretty writing” that costs clarity.
- Long abstract monologues detached from action.
- Explaining what the reader can infer from events.
- Dense techno-babble without immediate stakes or consequences.
- Mystery that only withholds; mystery should also **give** (partial answers, new angles, sharper questions).

---

## Revision Filters
Cut or reshape anything that doesn’t do at least one:
- move the plot
- sharpen a question
- pay off a curiosity
- reveal character pressure
- change the plan
- deepen the reader’s mental model of the world

If a paragraph is “nice” but does none of these, it’s a luxury item.

---

## One-Line Mantra
**Clarity first; curiosity always; thought-process as motion.**
