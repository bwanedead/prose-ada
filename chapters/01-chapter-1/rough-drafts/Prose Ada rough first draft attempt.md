# **Prose Ada**

hymn-centric

Chapter 1 breakfast

The gurgles of the last drops of water being pumped, along with the aroma of a fresh brewed pot of coffee filled my warm lit kitchen. Every time I hear that sound I think about upgrading—maybe one of those machines with a scheduled brewing feature—but something about my old pot was comforting. Like a dog associating the clicker with a treat, I'd built an unconscious bond between those gurgles and a dose of caffeine.

Those final drops overlapped with the murmur of TV news drifting from the other room—typical political bullshit. A panel of portraits parceled into a row of boxes, each passionately arguing past one another. The outrage, designed to be contagious, seemed unable to overwhelm my immunity. I rarely cared about such discussions and arguments, even paying attention felt forced.

I always scrape a knife over the top of my two chocolate chip waffles, testing their crispiness, before placing a square of salted grass-fed butter on top to melt gently into each crevice.

Pouring hot liquid makes a deeper rich sound than when it’s cold—a subtle difference I always notice while filling my cup, steam curling upward. A splash of cream until it's just the right shade of tan.

The butter square was nearly liquid now, and I spread the last semi-solid slivers across the waffles. Carefully, tracking the rows with a bottle labeled with a cozy northern pond and a goose stretching its wings, I filled each square indent with a full pour of syrup.

I settled into the worn leather upholstery of my armchair, steam rising from my coffee. Outside the window, the sky was dimming, a rich red hue forming on the horizon as the sun slipped behind distant pine trees. A storm was forecasted to roll through later tonight, bringing heavy winds from the north, but for now, everything seemed perfectly still. Hopefully, the clear skies would hold long enough for the telescope to collect usable data. Our backlog of observation requests stretched practically to eternity, so every bit of uptime was precious. Tonight we were scheduled to aim toward a star about thirty light-years away, trying to catch the transit of an exoplanet for some grant-funded research team studying average orbital periods. They’d probably waited in the queue for over a year already.

I stared out the window after taking the last delicious bite of my meal wishing I had toasted a couple more waffles, not to satiate my hunger, but just to have a couple more moments of warm sugary syrup coat my mouth. I still had my coffee to sip. I could waste a few moments before I had to break my trance and commute to the observatory.

I liked to hold the mug against my cheek and feel the warmth radiate into my flesh. While taking my last few moments staring out at the setting sun, the sky was growing with even richer reds and pinks. My mind was mostly blank the only thoughts were dread of getting up from this chair and enduring the cold January evening air, especially when I was so comfortable and content in this moment.

My attention drifted from this dread when I noticed the sparse, rosy clouds curled in a peculiar way. They still appeared as soft cotton-ball plumes, their patterns and subtle creases familiar enough that I must have been tracking their shapes subconsciously. Despite seeming unchanged, the clouds felt as though they were subtly drifting toward me. Yet each time I shifted my gaze, my immediate focal point confirmed the sky was perfectly still. Only when my attention relaxed for a moment did my peripheral vision register a subtle bulbing effect in the sky, as if a fish-eye lens was gently widening those familiar patterns at the edges while the center remained static.

I couldn't quite pinpoint the source of the distortion, only that somewhere deep in my mind, something signaled an inconsistency between expectation and perception. I found myself drawn in, mentally hunting for the exact feature triggering my puzzlement—like playing an odd game of "Where's Waldo?" with my own senses.

Instinctively, I squeezed my eyes shut to reset my vision, scrubbing them briefly with my fingers. When I opened them again, the fish-eye distortion returned, the sky seeming to steadily expand, reminiscent of that cinematic audio illusion created by staggered, looping tones that build endless anticipation. Then suddenly, the effect snapped off, leaving the sky unchanged, completely still, identical to a moment earlier—but now the illusion refused to return. It felt exactly like one of those optical tricks where once your brain flips the image, you can't force it to revert again, no matter how hard you try.

"Fuck," I muttered. "I'm going to be late."
