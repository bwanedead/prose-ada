The clock was the only thing in the room already awake.

17:08

Ada let the alarm sound twice before she reached out from under the blanket and silenced it. Not because she was tempted to go back to sleep. She liked the first few seconds uncommitted. The room told the truth if you listened before you started moving around in it.

Dark. Cool air above the covers. Left shoulder warm where she'd been folded into it too long. Dry mouth. One foot numb.

All workable.

She kept her eyes on the red digits until they changed, then pushed the blanket down and sat up. The mattress gave under her weight with the same soft complaint it always made near the wall. Somewhere in the house, a board answered a second later, as if the place had registered that she was on schedule.

Her bedroom still had the shape of morning even when the hour didn't. The blackout shades made sure of that. So did the lamp she never turned on in here, and the fact that waking always felt like waking no matter what the clock insisted. For a few breaths she could pretend the world outside was pale and just beginning. Then the thin bar of gray light under the shade reminded her that the day had already spent most of itself somewhere else.

She swung her legs over the side of the bed and found the rug with her toes. The corner nearest the frame had curled up again. She nudged it flat, knowing it would rise back as soon as she took her foot away.

Tape it down, she thought.

She had been meaning to tape it down since January.

The hallway beyond her room was narrow enough that she could cross it without quite waking all the way up. She did it by memory, one hand brushing the wall, and stepped into the living room before turning on the lamp by the window.

Amber light opened the apartment in pieces.

First the blue chair with the faded arm, its fabric worn smooth where hands had rested over the years. Then the small table beside it, stacked with two journals, a library book on comet history, and the coaster she kept meaning to throw away because the cork had split clean through. Then the shelf under the far wall, where mugs hung from hooks and one of them sat crooked because the hook had bent and she had never bothered to fix it.

The place was clean, but it had no interest in looking arranged. It looked used. A jacket over the back of the chair because last night she had been too tired to hang it up. A ceramic bowl by the door with keys in it and a lip that had been glued back together so neatly you only saw the crack if the light hit sideways. A pair of boots tucked under the bench, toes dusted with old salt from the last storm that had needed scraping but not yet earned it.

That was better than pristine. Pristine felt temporary. This felt occupied.

She stood still long enough for her eyes to stop treating the room as a rumor. The lamp heat touched one side of her face. The vent by the floor gave its intermittent tin rattle. The refrigerator hummed with the weary steadiness of an appliance that had decided long ago that excellence was too much to ask.

No voices. No traffic. No television through the wall. Just the little body-sounds of a house keeping its own company.

Good.

In the kitchen she filled the kettle from the sink. The pipes gave their quick metal shudder, then settled. Water struck steel. The faucet squeaked off. Quiet returned in layers.

She set the kettle on the burner and sparked the flame underneath it. Blue ring. Small hiss. Stable.

Coffee next.

She took the grounds down from the cabinet and measured them into the French press by habit more than counting. Enough for the mug she wanted now and half another later if the night became stupid. The glass was still faintly warm from having been washed and left near the stove to dry. She liked that. It made the sequence feel continuous, as if last night's version of her had left a courtesy for this one.

Some people woke up by accident and had to be dragged into the day. Ada preferred a procedure. Not a grand one. Just a short chain of actions that removed the burden of personality before work. Water. Flame. Grounds. Mug. Hair tie. Shower. Badge. Keys.

Mercy disguised as discipline.

The kettle began to mutter. She rinsed her mug with hot tap water and set it on the counter to warm. It was the heavier of the two she used most often, cream-colored once, now a permanent shade closer to weathered bone, with a crackle in the glaze near the handle that never deepened and never went away. The handle fit her hand cleanly. That mattered more than appearance.

She leaned one hip against the counter and waited for the water to rise toward a boil.

This was the best part of the shift, or one of them. Before the observatory screens. Before logs and signal noise and status lights and calibration drift. Before deciding whether a weird line in a set of data meant something or merely wanted to waste an hour of her life. Here, everything had a right answer. The burner was either on or it wasn't. The water was either hot enough or it wasn't. The coffee would either come out too bitter because she'd left it too long or it wouldn't. A closed system with decent manners.

The kettle hissed sharply. She poured.

The grounds lifted, darkened, opened. The first smell that came up was soft and almost sweet, then darker and sharper underneath. She let it bloom while she watched the surface settle. Steam passed her face and warmed the bridge of her nose.

Outside, a gull cried once. The sound came through muted, as if the bird had called from the far side of a blanket.

She smiled without deciding to.

There were people in town who found the gulls irritating year-round, even when half of them had retreated inland. To Ada they were useful. A small reminder that the coast was close even when the ocean itself stayed out of sight. Most days she didn't need the water visible. The air kept enough of it.

When the coffee was ready, she pressed the plunger down slowly, listening to the fine scrape of mesh through liquid. Then she carried the mug to the table by the lamp and sat with one leg tucked under her.

Too hot, the first sip told her.

She waited, tried again.

Better.

Across the room, her work bag rested where she'd left it after the last shift, leaning against the wall under the coat hooks. Laptop inside. Notebook in the front pocket. Badge clipped where she could see it. Packing early was an act of self-respect. Packing at the last minute was how one arrived at work without a charger, a notebook, or whatever other single object the night would then shape itself around punishing.

She looked toward the window.

The shades were down, but a narrow line of light showed at the bottom where one corner never quite sat flush. It cut a pale strip across the floorboards. Late-day light, not morning light. Flatter than dawn. Less forgiving. The kind of light that made a room seem aware of how much time had already happened without it.

Most people would be slowing down about now. Heading home. Checking what needed thawing for dinner. Deciding whether they had enough patience left for one errand or none.

She was still assembling into usefulness.

It rarely felt strange from the inside. Only when someone asked what her hours were in the tone people used for dietary restrictions or old injuries.

She set the mug down and went to the bathroom.

The tile was cold. The mirror had kept a faint haze from the last shower because the fan worked only when it felt flattered into it. She turned on the water, waited for the pipes to argue briefly with the temperature change, then pulled her hair up while the stream warmed.

When she stepped under it, the first heat at the back of her neck loosened something in her shoulders she hadn't known she was carrying. That was another mercy of routine: the body gave up its little stubbornnesses in the same order each day. Scalp. Neck. Spine. Jaw.

By the time she shut the water off, she was fully present.

She dressed in the clothes she reached for most shifts without thinking about them much: dark jeans, charcoal shirt, thick socks, sweater, then the heavier jacket she'd need once she stepped outside. Practical choices, all of them. Not because she was indifferent to appearance, but because work asked enough decisions of a person without requiring fashion to audition as one more.

She clipped her badge to her collar, took it off again, and wiped a faint water spot from the plastic with the hem of her shirt before clipping it back where it belonged.

In the kitchen she rinsed the press and left it upside down in the rack. She checked the burner once, then again. The second check was habit. The third, if it came, meant she was distracted. Tonight there was no third.

Her phone lay faceup on the counter with the screen dark. No messages from the observatory. No missed calls. Two update reminders she would continue to ignore until the language became openly threatening.

Fine.

She slipped the phone into her pocket, went back for the mug, and took the last swallow standing up.

The room looked different once she was dressed to leave. Smaller, maybe. Not less warm, just more obviously behind her. The lamp by the window threw its light over the chair, the shelf, the bowl of keys, the little geography of objects that had already done their part. From here on, the sequence belonged to the outside world.

She put the mug in the sink and wiped the faint ring it had left on the table with the side of her hand.

Keys. Bag. Jacket zipped. Badge checked.

She paused at the door with her fingers around the knob.

There was always a brief pocket here, half a breath long, when the apartment seemed sealed off from consequence. Lamp light at her back. Quiet holding steady in the corners. Every familiar object exactly where it ought to be. The life inside arranged well enough to release her into the next one.

Then she opened the door and the outside air came in cold and damp, carrying pine, wet pavement, and the faint mineral edge of the coast.

Ada stepped out, pulled the door shut behind her, and headed for the car.
