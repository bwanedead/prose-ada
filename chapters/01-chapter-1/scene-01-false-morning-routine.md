# Scene 01 — False-Morning Routine

## Intent

Onboard the reader into Ada's lived world with low-friction, texture-bearing atmosphere.
Deliver comfort, voice intimacy, and routine competence before stronger wrongness appears.

## Draft

The red digits were the only thing in the room that looked sure of itself.

17:08.

Ada listened to the alarm for two full pulses before she reached for it. Not because she needed time to wake up. Because she liked to know what state she was in before she started touching the world.

Dark room. Warm blanket. Dry throat. Left foot asleep where it had curled under the right.

All right, she thought. We are in one piece.

She killed the alarm and lay still for another breath, staring at the clock until the numbers changed. Then she pushed the blanket down and sat up. The air was cool enough to raise a line of gooseflesh on her forearms.

Her bedroom always did this trick at shift start. It felt like the beginning of a normal morning: dark walls, quiet house, body slow and heavy and vaguely annoyed at being negotiated with. But somewhere beyond the blackout shades, the day was finishing, not beginning.

She stood, felt for the floor with her toes, and found the worn edge of the rug where it had curled up near the bed frame. She made a note for the sixth time this month to tape it down, then stepped over it like she always did.

The hallway was a narrow strip of shadow. She crossed it by memory and clicked on the lamp in the living room instead of the overhead switch.

Amber light opened the room in layers.

The old blue chair by the window first, one arm polished pale where hands had rested for years. Then the low shelf with two chipped mugs and a stack of astronomy journals bent from being read in bed. Then the coffee table with a hairline crack in the wood that looked, in this light, like a map of a river she had never seen.

The place was clean in the way of someone who used things and put them back. Shoes tucked under the bench by the door. Jacket on the hook. Keys in the bowl with the ceramic lip she had glued twice. Dust mostly winning on the top shelf where she never looked unless the lamp caught it at an angle like this.

She rubbed her eyes with the heels of her hands and stood there a moment while the room came into focus and her body agreed to participate.

From the kitchen, she filled the kettle at the sink. The pipes answered with their usual small complaint. Water against metal. Faucet off. Silence returning in pieces.

She set the kettle on the burner, struck the lighter, and watched the ring of blue settle under it. On the counter, she measured grounds by touch into the French press: one rounded spoon, two, three and a little because she had a long night.

The house held itself around those small sounds. The thin rattle of the vent near the floor. The refrigerator motor making up its mind and changing pitch. Somewhere outside, a gull called once and then thought better of it.

She took her mug from the rack, the heavy one with the cracked glaze near the handle, and warmed it with hot tap water while the kettle climbed toward steam.

This was the part she liked. No messages yet. No graphs. No one talking. Just sequence.

Kettle hiss. Pour. Bloom. Wait.

The smell came up dark and sweet, almost chocolate at first, then sharper. She pressed the plunger slowly, listening to the faint scrape of mesh.

Ada carried the mug to the small table by the living room lamp and sat with one leg tucked under her. She took the first sip too soon, flinched, waited, then tried again.

Better.

Across the room, her work bag leaned against the wall where she had left it after last shift. Laptop. Field notebook. Badge lanyard clipped to the outer pocket. She could have packed in the morning, but if she packed in the morning she forgot things. Night shift had taught her that routines were less about discipline than mercy.

She took another sip and looked toward the window.

The shades were down tight, but a thin line of light leaked along the bottom edge and drew a pale bar across the floorboards. Late-day light. Flat and gray.

Most people started winding down around now. She was stepping into her day.

It never felt strange until she had to explain it to someone.

She set the mug down, stood, and moved through the rest of the sequence.

Bathroom light. Cold tile. Shower on. She waited for the water to heat while she tied her hair up, then stepped in and let the stream hit the back of her neck until her shoulders stopped pretending they were stone.

Shampoo, rinse, done.

She dressed in the uniform she never called a uniform: charcoal long-sleeve, dark jeans, thick socks, boots that had seen three Maine winters and looked like they expected a fourth. She clipped her badge to her belt loop, then unclipped it and moved it to the collar of her jacket where it belonged.

Back in the kitchen, she rinsed the press, left it upside down in the rack, and checked the stove twice out of habit and once because she did not trust habit.

Her phone sat face down on the counter. One message from an unknown number with no preview. Two software update reminders she would ignore until they became threatening. Nothing urgent from the observatory.

Good.

She slid the phone into her pocket, took her mug for one last sip, and let the quiet settle again.

In the quiet, she could hear the ocean if she paid attention. Not waves, exactly. More a low pressure in the distance, like the town had a second pulse under it.

She set the mug in the sink and wiped a ring of coffee from the table with the edge of her sleeve.

Keys. Bag. Jacket.

She checked the badge once more, because forgetting it meant a call to security and a ten-minute speech from Kevin about procedures, and she preferred uncertainty to Kevin certainty.

At the door, she paused with her hand on the knob.

There was always a short moment here, before the outside air, where the house felt almost sealed off from consequence. Lamp light behind her. Quiet room. Familiar objects exactly where they should be.

Then she opened the door and the evening cold moved in, carrying wet asphalt and pine and the faint salt of the coast.

Ada stepped out, pulled the door shut, and turned toward the car.
