[[[beat-01-wake-dark-room|Wake in the Dark Room|start]]]

Nothing showed at first.

The room was there, but not in any way that could be named yet. No edges. No objects. Just the closed dark and the stillness inside it.

It held for a few seconds like that.

Then a dim red glow began to separate itself.

Not enough to light the room. Just enough to interrupt it.

The numbers came first, blurred by distance and sleep, then the faint wash around them, a low red spill that touched the blanket and thinned across what lay beyond it. A patch of skin. The curve of something still not fully clear. Then the shine of an eye, already open.

Ada was watching the clock.

She had been watching it long enough that the numbers no longer felt like something to read. Just something to wait on.

The red reflection shifted across her eye.

Then the alarm began.

She let it sound twice before reaching out from under the blanket to silence it.

Not because she wanted more sleep. She liked the first few seconds before moving. Before the bed gave her back to the room. Before the small facts of herself started arriving one by one.

The warmth under the blanket had settled deep in around her. Outside it, the air sat cool against her face. One shoulder carried the dull complaint of having taken too much of her weight through the night. Her mouth was dry. One foot had drifted into the cold part of the bed.

She stayed where she was and let all of that come in without answering any of it yet.

Then she looked back at the clock, but she didn’t really read it. She never needed the exact minute at first. It was enough to know the sequence had begun.

The shade over the window kept most of the outside back. Only a thin seam of gray showed at the bottom where it didn’t quite meet the sill.

She pushed the blanket down and sat up.

The mattress gave under her weight with the same soft complaint it always made near the wall. Somewhere deeper in the apartment, the floor answered with a creak. The place always seemed to register her in little delayed acknowledgments, as if the boards and pipes kept their own version of her habits.

She sat there a moment longer, elbows on her knees.

Then she swung her legs over the side of the bed and found the rug with her toes. The corner nearest the bed had folded over on itself again. She pressed it flat, knowing it would be curled again later.

She stood carefully, not because she thought she might fall, but because waking had its own order and it paid to respect it. Weight into the feet first. Then knees. Then the rest of the body following without complaint.

The air above the rug felt cooler than it had under the blanket. She reached for the old cardigan draped over the chair by the bed and pulled it on without bothering to turn on the bedroom light. She never used the overhead unless she had dropped something small and urgent. Light from above was too abrupt this early in her sequence. It made the room feel interrogated.

The hallway beyond her door was narrow and unlit, more passage than room. She moved through it with one hand brushing the wall, the plaster cool against her fingers, her steps finding the boards she knew were quieter than the others.

The living room lamp clicked on with a dry little snap.

Amber light spread outward, slow enough that the room seemed to return in stages instead of appearing all at once.

The chair by the window came first, blue once and now some softened version of it, the arm rubbed pale where hands had found the same resting place for years. Then the shelf under the far wall. Then the small table beside the chair, stacked with journals, a folded receipt, and the library book she'd renewed twice because she kept reading three pages at a time and then getting distracted by whatever sky map or maintenance log had claimed the rest of the evening.

The apartment was clean, but not arranged into performance. A jacket lay over the back of the chair because she'd been tired when she came home. One boot sat neatly under the bench by the door and the other had missed by a few inches. A ceramic bowl held her keys and spare change under a repaired crack that only showed if the light hit across it instead of into it.

That was the part she liked.

Nothing here tried to pretend a life wasn't happening in it.

She stood still a moment and let her eyes finish adjusting. The vent near the floor gave a thin metallic rattle and then stopped. The refrigerator held its low, patient hum from the kitchen. Somewhere in the wall, a pipe ticked once as if reconsidering the temperature.

Beyond the drawn shade, late-day light worked at the edges of the room. A narrow gray seam under the window blind cut across the floorboards and stopped just short of the rug. Not dawn. Not even close. But inside, with the lamp on and the house still mostly silent, the body could be forgiven for thinking otherwise.

That was the trick of these hours. The room felt like morning even when the world outside was already letting go of the day.

Ada crossed to the window and touched two fingers to the edge of the shade without lifting it. Cool fabric. A hint of cold from the glass behind it. She could have looked out, but she didn't need the proof. The room and the light leaking under the sill had already settled the argument.

She let the shade fall back into place and glanced around once more, not checking exactly, just taking inventory in the loose way one does of things that matter because they keep being there. Chair. Table. Shelf. Bag still by the wall where she'd left it after the last shift. Everything holding.

Good.

The kitchen could come next.
